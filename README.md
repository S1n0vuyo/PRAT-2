# PRAT-2
Morse code is a communication system invented in the 1830s by Samuel F. B. Morse and Alfred Vail, using sequences of dots and dashes to represent letters, numbers, and symbols. It was created to enable long-distance communication via the telegraph, revolutionizing how information traveled across nations. The system works by encoding characters into short signals (dots) and long signals (dashes), transmitted through sound, light, or electrical pulses

Historical Context. Invention: Developed in the 1830s by Samuel Morse, an artist and inventor, with assistance from Alfred Vail. Motivation: At the time, long-distance communication was slow—letters could take days or weeks. Morse’s personal tragedy (learning of his wife’s death too late to attend her funeral) highlighted the need for faster communication. Impact: Telegraph lines spread rapidly across the U.S. and Europe, transforming commerce, journalism, and military operations. Global Adoption: By 1851, European nations standardized International Morse Code, making it suitable for multiple languages.

How It Works
•	Signals: Each character is represented by a unique sequence of dots (short signals) and dashes (long signals).
•	Timing Rules:
o	A dot lasts one unit of time.
o	A dash lasts three units.
o	Spaces between parts of a letter = one unit.
o	Spaces between letters = three units.
o	Spaces between words = seven units.
•	Transmission Methods:
o	Electrical pulses via telegraph wires.
o	Sound signals (beeps or clicks).
o	Visual signals (flashing lights or flags).
